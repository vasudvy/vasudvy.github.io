When I think about the fractured nature of identity, I'm reminded of Freud's psychosexual development theory - though modern psychology has largely abandoned this framework. Freud believed our personality develops through five distinct stages: the Oral stage (0-1 years) where pleasure is derived through the mouth, sucking and biting; the Anal stage (1-3 years) where control and pleasure come from bowel movements and toilet training; the Phallic stage (3-6 years) where children discover their genitals and develop unconscious sexual desires for the opposite-sex parent; the Latency stage (6-puberty) where sexual feelings are dormant and children focus on developing skills and friendships; and finally the Genital stage (puberty onwards) where mature sexual interests emerge.

Though contemporary psychology has moved beyond these rigid stages, what strikes me isn't their accuracy but the underlying assumption that our identity is formed through these early fragmentations of pleasure and pain, satisfaction and denial. Every being is confined within the limits of its own sensory system, experiencing not the world as it is, but a filtered, species-specific version of it. A human perceives reality in colors determined by the visible light spectrum (wavelengths between 400–700 nm). A bird sees ultraviolet patterns invisible to humans, a snake detects infrared heat signatures, and a bat constructs its world through echolocation. None of these realities are false, but none are complete either.

What we call "the world" is merely the result of a biological filtering function. If reality itself is an infinite-dimensional structure (let’s call it R), then each species experiences only a fraction of it. Since perception is designed for survival rather than truth, each function discards vast portions of reality that are not biologically useful.

We might assume that by combining perspectives, we get closer to full reality. If a human and a bird share their perceptions, their combined reality would be more complete than either one alone, but still only a fragment of R. Even if we extend this to all species—combining every sensory system from eagles to whales, from insects to fungi—we still do not arrive at R, the true reality. Instead, we get:

Sum of all biological perception = S_bio (still incomplete)

This tells us something profound: No matter how many perspectives we merge, we will never fully reconstruct reality. Even the totality of what all species combined can perceive is still an approximation, like a shadow of a higher-dimensional truth.

If we wanted to escape these limitations, we would need to "invert" perception—to take the sum of all sensed realities and reconstruct the full R. But perception functions are lossy—they throw away information irreversibly. Just as a two-dimensional shadow cannot fully describe the three-dimensional object that cast it, our sensory experiences cannot fully describe R.

This suggests that reality exists in a higher-order space beyond perception. Even if we extend our senses with technology—infrared cameras, X-ray telescopes, particle detectors—these are just tools that stretch our biological filters a little further. They don’t dissolve the fundamental limitation.

For every possible observer, there will always be a part of reality that remains inaccessible.

No perspective, no sum of perspectives, and no technological advancement can ever fully grasp R. If all perception is just a projection of R, then no being, no matter how advanced, ever experiences the full truth. This is why reality is not a fixed, knowable object, but an ever-receding horizon. It is something we can move toward but never fully reach.

Sum of all perspectives → R (but never reaches it)

This is why perception is both necessary and deceptive—it gives us just enough to survive, but not enough to see things as they really are.

> All perception is a shadow, and all reality is a projection from an unreachable dimension.

We do not see the world as it is. We see the world as our biology allows us to. The full truth remains forever beyond our grasp—but it is in the search for that truth that meaning emerges.

Let reality, denoted as R, exist in an infinite-dimensional space where n → ∞. Each species perceives only a subset of this reality, represented as S_i, where S_i is a projection of R filtered through the biological and cognitive constraints of that species. Mathematically, this relationship can be described by a perception function P_i, such that:

P_i(R) = S_i

This function is inherently lossy, meaning it discards vast amounts of information from R, leaving only a compressed, survival-relevant fraction of reality. As a consequence, the dimensionality of any species' perceptual world is strictly less than the dimensionality of full reality:

dim(S_i) < dim(R)

Even when multiple species' perceptions are combined, they still fail to reconstruct R in its entirety. If a human and a bird merge their sensory data, their collective perceptual world can be written as:

S_h + S_b = S_hb This new space S_hb is richer than either individual’s perception, yet it remains a projection of R, rather than R itself. Extending this summation to include all biological perception, we define:

S_bio = Σ P_i(R) for all species i

This represents the totality of all known perceptual systems. However, since perception functions are non-invertible—meaning they irreversibly discard information—attempting to reconstruct R from S_bio is impossible:

P_i⁻¹ (S_i) ≠ R

This guarantees that even the sum of all biological perception remains incomplete:

S_bio ⊂ R, but S_bio ≠ R

The question then arises: can artificial sensors and technological extensions of perception bridge the gap? If we define S_tech as the additional perceptual data gained through machines (such as infrared, X-rays, radio waves), we obtain a more expanded perceptual space:

S_total = S_bio + S_tech But even as technology improves indefinitely, the fundamental limitation remains. The perceptual functions that shape both biological and artificial observation are still projections of R, not R itself. Thus, the asymptotic limit of expanding knowledge is:

lim (S_total) as tech → ∞ ≠ R

The Fundamental Mismatch Problem

One of the most striking contradictions in human existence is the mismatch between the exponential evolution of our environment and the near-static nature of our biology. The lungs of a prehistoric human and those of a modern human are functionally identical, yet the air they breathe, the pollutants they encounter, and the lifestyle demands imposed upon them have changed drastically. This mismatch extends far beyond physiology—our cognitive functions, stress responses, and sensory systems remain largely tuned to an ancestral world, even as we navigate a reality shaped by agriculture, industrialization, and now, rapid technological acceleration.

If we map this onto a mathematical model, we define B(t) as the function representing biological evolution over time and E(t) as the function representing environmental change. Human biology evolves on a slow, near-linear or logarithmic timescale, constrained by genetic drift and natural selection:

B(t) ≈ c (constant over short timescales)

In contrast, environmental evolution—driven by technological advancement, population growth, and cultural shifts—follows an exponential trajectory:

E(t) ≈ e^t

The difference between these two functions defines a growing mismatch over time:

Δ(t) = E(t) - B(t)

Initially, in prehistoric times, Δ(t) ≈ 0 because environmental demands and biological evolution were synchronized. However, as human civilization advanced through agriculture, industry, and digital technology, E(t) began outpacing B(t) at an increasing rate. The gap between what our biology was designed for and what our environment now demands has widened dramatically:

lim (E(t) - B(t)) as t → ∞ = ∞

This widening delta explains many modern health crises—our bodies and minds, optimized for small tribal groups, high-activity lifestyles, and natural diets, are now forced to adapt to dense urbanization, sedentary work, ultra-processed foods, and digital overstimulation. The biological stress response, once a survival mechanism against predators, now misfires in the face of email notifications and financial pressures. Our dopamine systems, designed for intermittent rewards, are now bombarded by the hyper-stimulation of social media, leading to addiction-like behaviors.

This mismatch has consequences beyond individual health; it shapes the very structure of societies. Political systems struggle to regulate technologies that evolve faster than governance can adapt. Education systems designed for the industrial age fail to prepare students for the digital era. Even our collective psychology is destabilized by a reality changing at speeds that outpace cognitive adaptation.

The fundamental question, then, is whether human evolution can ever catch up with the exponential curve of E(t). Biologically, the answer is no—evolutionary change occurs over millennia, whereas technological revolutions happen in decades. The only potential resolution is not biological adaptation, but rather self-directed augmentation—the merging of biology with technology through bioengineering, neural interfaces, and AI integration.

In essence, the future of human existence is not about catching up to environmental change but about changing the very nature of what it means to be human.
